# AI imagery policy

The standing rules for generative imagery on UNDRR web properties. Read this page before generating any AI imagery for UNDRR work. For copy-paste prompt templates that put these rules into practice, see <LinkTo kind="brand-ai-imagery-prompts" story="docs">AI imagery prompts</LinkTo>.

<DocCallout variant="warning">
  <strong>Experimental guidance.</strong> These rules are working policy derived from the institutional sources below, not a settled UNDRR-wide standard. Generative tools change frequently and the same prompt produces different results over time. Confirm the current version with UNDRR Communications (`undrrcomms@un.org`) and your office's AI focal point before publishing AI-assisted work.
</DocCallout>

This page is derivative of UNDRR's institutional AI guidance, not a one-to-one match of it. If SharePoint policy and this page disagree at any point, SharePoint policy wins.

## Standing rules

<DocCallout variant="warning">
  <strong>Rules 1–5 are absolute prohibitions with no exceptions.</strong> No photorealistic humans, no named people or events, no identifiable faces, no AI imagery of children at all, no rendered text or icons in AI outputs.
</DocCallout>

1. **No photorealistic AI of human beings.** AI imagery may depict people only in clearly stylised forms — flat vector, line illustration, editorial illustration. The output must not read as photographic. This aligns with AP, Reuters, and Guardian editorial standards on AI in journalism, and with the BBC's permissive lane for stylised graphics. For real publication imagery of real people, commission credited photography.
1. **Never use AI imagery for named people or specific real events.** Use AI for editorial illustration, conceptual diagrams, infographic backgrounds, and concept exploration only.
1. **Never instruct a model to generate identifiable human faces.** Models cannot generate "named individuals" — they fabricate realistic faces that may match real people in their training data, without consent. Even in stylised illustration, ask for faces in profile, three-quarter view, in motion, or without rendered detail.
1. **Never generate AI imagery of children, identifiable or not.** Even stylised AI children invite scrutiny — the model has no way to obtain guardian consent. For child-focused topics, choose one of: illustration without human figures (settings, infrastructure, schoolyards empty); illustration of adult caregivers, teachers, or community leaders only; or commission credited photography with documented guardian consent per UNICEF child-safeguarding standards.
1. **No rendered text or standalone icons in AI outputs.** AI image models render text as gibberish (mangled letters that look like writing but aren't), and they invent icons that drift from the OCHA + Mangrove icon systems. Every prompt should include "no text, no rendered letters, no logos, no icons." If signage is conceptually needed in the frame, request "pictogram-only signs without rendered words." Add real text, captions, and icons in your design tool (Figma, Illustrator) _after_ generation, never inside the AI output.
1. **Specify a named country, not a region.** "Pacific Islands" or "sub-Saharan Africa" produce cultural pastiche. Pick one named place per generation (for example, "Vanuatu, Efate Island," "Kenya, Mombasa County") and a specific built-environment cue.
1. **Diversity within a named place — name what kind of gathering this is, and never use this rule to render resident minorities invisible.** The named-country rule and the diversity rule pull against each other in single-country contexts: when you name "Bangladesh," the model defaults to a single ethnic phenotype even when you ask for varied skin tones. Resolve this by being explicit about the type of gathering — but always against the actual demographic composition of the named locality, including resident minorities.
   - **Local community context** (a single village, a specific community-led activity): match the actual demographic composition of the named locality, including resident minorities. Ethnic homogeneity is acceptable only where the named locality is genuinely homogeneous; it must never be used to render resident minorities invisible (Rohingya in Bangladesh, Uyghurs in Xinjiang, Roma across Europe, Indigenous peoples in any settler-state context). Diversity within a homogeneous locality should come through age, gender, ability, body size, and role.
   - **National-government context in a country with significant within-country diversity**: name the heritage groups explicitly. For Bangladesh: "Bengali, and Adivasi peoples including Chakma, Marma, Garo, Santal." For Brazil: "Afro-Brazilian, Indigenous, European-Brazilian, and Asian-Brazilian heritage — including visibly Indigenous participants where the named context plausibly includes them." For Indonesia: "Javanese, Sundanese, Batak, Bugis, Papuan and other heritage groups." Ask for the diversity that actually exists in that place.
   - **Regional or international convening hosted in a named country**: name the participating countries. For example: "South Asian regional DRR planning meeting hosted in Dhaka, with participants from Bangladesh, Bhutan, India, Maldives, Nepal, Pakistan, and Sri Lanka."
   - **Religious minority visibility**: in named places with significant religious minorities, vary religious dress cues (hijab worn and not worn, kippah, turban, cross, no visible marker) rather than defaulting to majority signifiers.
1. **Disability inclusion is required, not optional, in every people-inclusive prompt — and scaled to the scene.** The Sendai Framework explicitly addresses disability-inclusive DRR. Treat "one disabled participant" as the floor for small scenes, not the ceiling.
   - **Scene-size floor:** at least one visibly disabled participant in any group of three to six; two or more in larger gatherings. Vary the type of disability across your output set, not within a single prompt.
   - **Rotate the disability type across outputs.** Pick from: mobility (wheelchair, crutches, prosthetic limb, mobility scooter, visible assistive footwear, walking stick); sensory (white cane, guide dog, hearing aid, cochlear implant visible behind the ear, sign language being used); invisible / chronic (medical alert bracelet, insulin pump visible at the waistband, oxygen cannula, walking stick used by a younger adult).
   - **Neurodivergence should be addressed through role and behaviour, not visual stereotypes.** Do not prompt for visual "autism" or "ADHD" iconography — models invent stereotyped cues that misrepresent neurodivergent people.
   - **Frame disabled participants as peers, not recipients.** A wheelchair user among community planners; a sign language interpreter at a briefing; a participant using a white cane near an early-warning siren as a drill leader, not as a person being led.
   - **Intersect with age.** Disability prevalence rises sharply over 60; disabled participants should not all be young adults. Pair disability with the older-adult floor in rule 10.
1. **Decolonial framing — and counter the model's named defaults.** Avoid "outside expert / local recipient" prompt structures. Frame people as peers with named professional roles. The unmarked party in a prompt is the one most likely to default to white/male in model output.
   - **No reverse stereotypes.** Do not flip the trope — avoid "visibly Western recipient / Global South expert" as a corrective. Both framings reduce people to nationality.
   - **No white-saviour postures.** Never prompt for a visibly Western or white participant in a "helping" posture (kneeling beside, demonstrating to, handing to). If a non-local technical advisor is required by the scene, frame them as listening or co-working at the same eye level.
   - **Counter the model's named defaults.** Models default to: African = rural, Asian = urban high-tech, Latin American = informal settlement, Middle Eastern = conflict zone, Pacific = beach. Counter-prompt explicitly: "urban Nairobi government office," "rural Japanese coastal village," "middle-class São Paulo neighbourhood," "Amman university campus," "Suva ministry building."
1. **Diversity must be specified, not implied — and varied across multiple axes.** "Diverse" alone is a weak token. Name what you want across each axis below.
   - **Skin tone** across the range relevant to the named place.
   - **Age** including older adults; **at least one participant aged 60+ in any multi-person scene, in an active role** — not in the background.
   - **Body size** including visibly fat / larger-bodied adults in professional roles, not only as background figures.
   - **Hair textures** including textured and curly hair worn naturally.
   - **Clothing** varied within regional norms — not all participants in identical dress.
1. **Indigenous self-determination requires extra care.** Do not depict ceremonial dress, sacred objects, body markings, tattoos, or regalia in AI output — these require community consent the model cannot obtain. Depict Indigenous people in everyday professional and community contexts (community planners, scientists, recovery practitioners, municipal staff). When the story is specifically about Indigenous-led practice (e.g. traditional fire management, watershed stewardship), commission credited photography or commissioned illustration through Indigenous artists; do not generate.
1. **LGBTQ+ identity cues must not create safety risk.** Do not add visual LGBTQ+ identity cues (Pride flags, hand-holding same-sex couples, explicit symbols) to scenes set in countries where this carries criminal or safety risk for real people who may be associated with the image. LGBTQ+ inclusion in inclusive imagery should come through gender expression and pairing diversity in contexts where this is safe.
1. **Use specific hex codes for colours.** "Sendai colours" alone means nothing to an image model. The full palette is: `#c10920` Sendai red, `#eb752a` Sendai orange, `#962987` Sendai purple, `#00afae` Sendai turquoise. Reduced-saturation versions: `#d45363`, `#f19e6a`, `#b669ab`, `#4dc7c6`. Plus `#004f91` UNDRR Corporate Blue. Sub-brand primaries: `#0a6969` PreventionWeb teal, `#591a61` MCR2030 purple, `#0f78bf` IRP bright blue, `#132e48` DELTA dark navy, `#921d2c` ARISE burgundy. Always include the actual hex values in prompts, not just the colour name.
1. **Label AI-generated imagery on publication.** UNDRR's editorial standard requires credit and source for every image; generative work surfaces its provenance the same way. [C2PA Content Credentials](https://contentcredentials.org) (cryptographic provenance manifests) are an emerging standard worth exploring as platform support grows; adopt opportunistically where the publishing tool supports it. This is not yet a UNDRR-wide commitment.

## When to use AI imagery (and when not to)

| Use AI imagery for | Do not use AI imagery for |
| --- | --- |
| Editorial illustration where no specific real person is depicted | News stories about named people |
| Conceptual diagrams (system explainers, framework illustrations) | Hero photography of real events |
| Infographic backgrounds | Crisis or response imagery from live disasters |
| Concept exploration before commissioning a real photographer or illustrator | Anywhere a credited photo of a real person and place would serve better |
| Stylised vector illustration of generic community settings | Identifiable portraits of any kind |
| Sub-brand prompt variants for explainer pages | Children, anywhere, in any rendered form |
| Adult-only community / professional scenes | Indigenous ceremonial or sacred contexts (commission instead) |

If the brief calls for documentary truth about a named person or event, commission credited photography. AI imagery is for the spaces where stylised illustration is appropriate.
