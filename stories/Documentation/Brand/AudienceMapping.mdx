# Audience mapping

UNDRR communicates with five distinct audiences. The voice is constant. The framing, the proof points, and the visual emphasis shift. This page is a short, practical reference for how each audience reads the same content differently.

The principle is from UNDRR's <LinkTo kind="brand-brand-guidelines" story="docs">brand guidelines</LinkTo>: "more targeted communication for different stakeholders." The page operationalises that.

---

## The five audiences

| Audience | Where they show up most | What they need | Primary register |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Governments and policy-makers** | undrr.org, Sendai Framework pages, GAR | Evidence, frameworks, references they can cite back | Authoritative |
| **Practitioners (DRR specialists, researchers)** | preventionweb.net, IRP, methodology pages | Toolkits, data, peer-reviewed outputs, applicability | Authoritative + Knowledgeable |
| **Local governments and community leaders** | mcr2030.undrr.org, regional pages | Worked examples, actionable steps, peer cities | Collaborative + Warm |
| **Private sector** | ariseglobalnetwork.org, partnership pages | Risk-reduction ROI, frameworks for action, peer companies | Authoritative + Hopeful |
| **Media, citizens, partners** | News, IDDRR, social, anniversary microsites | Narrative, stakes, what to do, who to follow | Warm + Hopeful + Urgent (when relevant) |

## How framing shifts (one story, five framings)

To make the principle concrete: the same story — community-led mangrove restoration in a Pacific Island nation — should be framed differently for each audience.

### For governments and policy-makers

> Coastal community restoration of 80 hectares of mangrove in Country X reduced storm-surge damage by 38% during Cyclone Y, per the 2024 post-event review (UNDRR / national disaster authority). The project illustrates Sendai Framework Priority 3 (investing in disaster risk reduction for resilience) at the sub-national level and is replicable under existing climate-adaptation finance instruments.

Emphasis: framework citation, replicability, evidence base, fiscal context.

### For practitioners

> Eighty hectares restored over 18 months, using a hybrid mangrove-and-seagrass design adapted from IRP guidance. Cost-benefit analysis, methodology, and monitoring data are available in the linked dataset. Comparable approaches are documented across three Pacific Island nations; this case adds quantified storm-surge attenuation under a Category 3 cyclone.

Emphasis: replicable method, comparative data, downloadable artifacts.

### For local governments and community leaders

> Talcahuano-Sur, a town of 12,000 on Country X's western coast, planted 80 hectares of mangrove with help from neighbouring communities and the national environment ministry. When Cyclone Y hit, storm-surge damage was a third lower than in the next bay over. The project plan, budget, and the names of partners are linked below — your municipality can adapt the same template.

Emphasis: peer recognition, actionable template, partner naming, real numbers.

### For private sector

> Eighty hectares of restored mangrove kept Country X's port operational through Cyclone Y — preventing an estimated USD 14 million in supply-chain disruption for member-state importers. The ARISE-aligned partnership behind the restoration includes shipping, insurance, and logistics partners whose continuity directly benefited. ROI and continuity-planning lessons are linked below.

Emphasis: continuity ROI, partnership model, peer companies, transferable lessons.

### For media, citizens, and partners

> Maria, 47, planted her first mangrove seedling in 2022 alongside her two daughters. By the time Cyclone Y reached the bay this March, those seedlings were three metres tall — and held. Talcahuano-Sur's port, school, and 40 nearby homes came through the storm with damage neighbours in unsheltered bays did not.

Emphasis: named people, specific moments, sensory detail, plain framing of outcome.

## Visual emphasis shifts

The framing shift is not only verbal. The same story warrants different visual emphasis per audience.

| Audience | Hero treatment | Supporting visuals | Tone of imagery |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Governments | Restrained — institutional photography or chart-led hero | Framework diagrams, summary charts | Authoritative |
| Practitioners | Method-led — diagrams, before/after maps, monitoring data | Detail shots of the work, methodology illustrations | Knowledgeable |
| Local governments | Peer-led — the people who did it, doing it | Worked examples from comparable cities | Warm + Collaborative |
| Private sector | Outcome-led — the asset that stayed operational | Partnership maps, ROI tables | Authoritative + Hopeful |
| Media, citizens | Story-led — named people in their place | Process imagery, candid moments | Warm + Hopeful |

> **Tip:** When in doubt, ask: "If this audience has 10 seconds, what is the single thing they should remember?" The answer drives both framing and hero choice.

---

## What does not change across audiences

These remain constant regardless of audience:

- Plain language. The Authoritative register is still plain — no jargon walls.
- Photo credits. Always.
- Sentence case headings. Always.
- Gender-neutral and disability-inclusive language. Always.
- Inverted-pyramid structure on the web. Always.
- The K / A / C voice trio. Always.
- The UNDRR logo lockup and Sendai Framework attribution. Always.

The brand thread is what makes a 10-second skim by a busy minister read as obviously UNDRR-family even though the framing is different from the 10-second skim a community leader does in another tab.
