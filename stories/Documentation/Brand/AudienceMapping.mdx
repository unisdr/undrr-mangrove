# Audience mapping

UNDRR communicates with many audiences. The voice is constant. The framing, the proof points, and the visual emphasis shift per audience. This page is a Mangrove-side practical reference for how each audience reads the same content differently.

> **What this page is, and is not.** This is an editorial simplification synthesised from UNDRR's [Strategic Framework 2026-2030](https://www.undrr.org/strategic-framework-2026-2030), the [Sendai Stakeholder Engagement Mechanism](https://www.undrr.org/implementing-sendai-framework/partners-and-stakeholders/stakeholder-engagement-mechanism), and the [Partners and stakeholders](https://www.undrr.org/partners-and-stakeholders) pages. For the canonical institutional stakeholder list — including parliamentarians, science and technology community (STAG), media, civil society, children and youth, regional intergovernmental organisations, and the 21 Major Groups under the SEM — see those sources. The cuts below are useful for web-framing decisions, not a complete UNDRR stakeholder model.

## Primary web audiences

These are the audiences who most commonly land on a UNDRR web property and need framing decisions on what they see first. The register column shows the *primary emphasis* for each — not an exclusive assignment. Per <LinkTo kind="brand-written-voice" story="docs">written voice</LinkTo>, Authoritative is the floor for all audiences. What shifts is which register leads.

| Audience | Where they show up most | What they need | Register emphasis |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Governments and policy-makers** (Member States, national disaster authorities, ministries) | undrr.org, Sendai Framework pages, GAR, Strategic Framework | Evidence, frameworks, references they can cite back | Authoritative + Diplomatic |
| **Practitioners** (DRR specialists, recovery planners) | preventionweb.net, recovery.preventionweb.net, Words into Action | Toolkits, data, peer-reviewed outputs, applicability | Authoritative + Knowledgeable + Procedural |
| **Science and technology community** (STAG, researchers, academia) | preventionweb.net, Strategic Framework, the GAR | Methodology, source data, citation-rich outputs, calls for evidence | Knowledgeable + Authoritative |
| **Local governments and community leaders** | mcr2030.undrr.org, regional pages | Worked examples, actionable steps, peer cities, Scorecard tooling | Collaborative + Warm |
| **Private sector — SMEs, member companies, regional networks** | ariseglobalnetwork.org, partnership pages | Continuity case, peer companies, member-network belonging, ROI | Authoritative + Hopeful + peer-network |
| **Civil society and NGOs** | preventionweb.net (consultations, calls), undrr.org partnerships | Convening, calls, partner directories, inclusion pathways | Collaborative + Authoritative |
| **Parliamentarians** | undrr.org parliamentary engagement track | Legislative levers, framework summaries, country-cited evidence | Authoritative + Diplomatic |
| **UN system and Resident Coordinators** | undrr.org cooperation framework pages, EW4All operational updates | Operational milestones, joint-programming hooks, attribution language | Authoritative + Procedural |
| **Media** (intermediaries — they amplify to citizens) | undrr.org/news, press releases | Quotable lines, named spokespeople, clear stakes, briefing-ready stats | Authoritative + Warm |
| **General public via campaigns** (IDDRR, anniversaries, social) | IDDRR microsites, anniversary pages, social channels | Narrative, stakes, what to do, who to follow | Warm + Hopeful + Time-sensitive (when relevant) |
| **Partners and donors** (institutional reciprocity — IFRC, Red Cross/Crescent, IFIs, donor states) | Partnership pages, GAR co-publications, anniversary acknowledgements | Reciprocal recognition, joint outcomes, transparent attribution | Diplomatic + Authoritative |

## Inclusion audiences (cross-cutting)

The Sendai Framework explicitly names **women**, **children and youth**, **persons with disabilities**, **older persons**, **Indigenous Peoples**, and **migrants and displaced persons** as audiences with distinct roles in DRR — not as topics. Each has a dedicated UNDRR programme or landing page. They are not a separate row in the table above because they intersect every other row: a national-government audience includes women and Indigenous decision-makers; a private-sector audience includes disabled SME owners and youth entrepreneurs; a community-leader audience includes older adults and migrant residents.

Treat inclusion as a cross-cutting requirement, not an afterthought:

- **Women.** Sendai Framework gender-equality commitments; UNDRR Women's Leadership programme. Surface women as decision-makers, not only as affected populations.
- **Children and youth.** [Children and youth landing page](https://www.undrr.org/implementing-sendai-framework/partners-and-stakeholders/children-and-youth). Voices-of-youth content; never AI imagery of children (see <LinkTo kind="brand-ai-imagery-policy" story="docs">AI imagery policy</LinkTo>).
- **Persons with disabilities.** Sendai Framework named role; Stakeholders Group at the Global Platform; disability-inclusion floor in the <LinkTo kind="brand-ai-imagery-policy" story="docs">AI imagery policy</LinkTo>.
- **Older persons.** Named in the Sendai Framework text; an explicit floor (at least one participant aged 60+ in any multi-person scene) in the AI imagery policy.
- **Indigenous Peoples.** Named in the Sendai Framework text; surface Indigenous practitioners as decision-makers, with commissioned imagery and consent for ceremonial or sacred content.
- **Migrants and displaced persons.** Named in the Sendai Framework; cross-references the [Inclusion page](https://www.undrr.org/implementing-sendai-framework/inclusion) and the IDMC partnership work.

Inclusion audiences should be visible in every primary-audience framing, not segregated into "inclusion content."

## How framing shifts (one story, five framings)

The exercise below is **pedagogical**, not editorial. The story is invented to demonstrate the principle; do not use invented protagonists in production. UNDRR's "real people, named" rule from <LinkTo kind="brand-brand-guidelines" story="docs">brand guidelines</LinkTo> means production copy must use the actual country, the actual event, and named real people (with their permission and proper credit).

> **For a real, multi-audience worked example, read [Pathways to Resilience: Stories from MCR2030 Cities](https://www.undrr.org/publication/pathways-to-resilience-stories-from-mcr2030-cities) (UNDRR, January 2026).** Pick one city case from the publication and walk it through the five framings below to see how the principle works on real material.

The illustrative story: community-led mangrove restoration in a Pacific Island nation. "Country X," "Cyclone Y," "Talcahuano-Sur," and "Maria" are placeholders — replace with real referents in production.

### For governments and policy-makers

> Coastal community restoration of 80 hectares of mangrove in Country X reduced storm-surge damage by 38% during Cyclone Y, per the 2024 post-event review (UNDRR / national disaster authority). The project illustrates Sendai Framework Priority 3 (investing in disaster risk reduction for resilience) at the sub-national level and is replicable under existing climate-adaptation finance instruments.

Emphasis: framework citation, replicability, evidence base, fiscal context.

### For practitioners

> Eighty hectares restored over 18 months, using a hybrid mangrove-and-seagrass design adapted from IRP guidance. Cost-benefit analysis, methodology, and monitoring data are available in the linked dataset. Comparable approaches are documented across three Pacific Island nations; this case adds quantified storm-surge attenuation under a Category 3 cyclone.

Emphasis: replicable method, comparative data, downloadable artifacts.

### For local governments and community leaders

> Talcahuano-Sur, a town of 12,000 on Country X's western coast, planted 80 hectares of mangrove with help from neighbouring communities and the national environment ministry. When Cyclone Y hit, storm-surge damage was a third lower than in the next bay over. The project plan, budget, and the names of partners are linked below — your municipality can adapt the same template.

Emphasis: peer recognition, actionable template, partner naming, real numbers.

### For private sector

> Eighty hectares of restored mangrove kept Country X's port operational through Cyclone Y — preventing an estimated USD 14 million in supply-chain disruption for member-state importers. The ARISE-aligned partnership behind the restoration includes shipping, insurance, and logistics partners whose continuity directly benefited. ROI and continuity-planning lessons are linked below.

Emphasis: continuity ROI, partnership model, peer companies, transferable lessons.

### For media, citizens, and partners

> Maria, 47, planted her first mangrove seedling in 2022 alongside her two daughters. By the time Cyclone Y reached the bay this March, those seedlings were three metres tall — and held. Talcahuano-Sur's port, school, and 40 nearby homes came through the storm with damage neighbours in unsheltered bays did not.

Emphasis: named people, specific moments, sensory detail, plain framing of outcome.

## Visual emphasis shifts

The framing shift is not only verbal. The same story warrants different visual emphasis per audience.

| Audience | Hero treatment | Supporting visuals | Tone of imagery |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Governments and policy-makers | Restrained — institutional photography or chart-led hero | Framework diagrams, summary charts | Authoritative |
| Practitioners | Method-led — diagrams, before/after maps, monitoring data | Detail shots of the work, methodology illustrations | Knowledgeable |
| Science and technology | Data-led — sourced charts, citation density visible | Methodology diagrams, dataset previews | Knowledgeable + Authoritative |
| Local governments | Peer-led — the people who did it, doing it | Worked examples from comparable cities | Warm + Collaborative |
| Private sector — SMEs | Outcome-led — the asset that stayed operational; SME owner in own workplace | Partnership maps, ROI tables, named regional chapters | Authoritative + Hopeful |
| Civil society and NGOs | Convening-led — multi-organisation meetings as peers | Partner directories, call-for-input artifacts | Collaborative |
| Parliamentarians | Briefing-led — country-cited summary cards | Country profile graphics, legislative-progress markers | Authoritative + Diplomatic |
| UN system / RCs | Joint-programming-led — co-branded artifacts | Operational status maps, attribution lockups | Authoritative + Procedural |
| Media | Briefing-ready — quotable hero with named spokesperson | Press-pack imagery, fact-box graphics | Authoritative + Warm |
| General public (campaigns) | Story-led — named people in their place | Process imagery, candid moments | Warm + Hopeful |
| Partners and donors | Attribution-led — co-branded outcomes; reciprocal recognition | Joint-publication covers, anniversary lockups | Diplomatic + Authoritative |

> **Tip:** When in doubt, ask: "If this audience has 10 seconds, what is the single thing they should remember?" The answer drives both framing and hero choice.

## What does not change across audiences

The threads documented in <LinkTo kind="brand-layered-identity" story="docs">layered identity</LinkTo> are constant regardless of audience: the K / A / C voice trio, plain language, sentence case, photo credits, gender-neutral and disability-inclusive language, inverted-pyramid structure, the UNDRR logo lockup with Sendai Framework attribution.

The brand thread is what makes a 10-second skim by a busy minister read as obviously UNDRR-family even though the framing is different from the 10-second skim a community leader does in another tab.
