# Sub-brand rationale

Why does each sub-brand look and sound different from the others, and what stays constant across all of them? This page articulates the audience, register, and visual emphasis that distinguishes PreventionWeb, MCR2030, IRP, ARISE, and DELTA Resilience — sitting on top of the core UNDRR thread documented in <LinkTo kind="brand-layered-identity" story="docs">layered identity</LinkTo>.

**On this page:**

- The constant thread (every sub-brand)
- PreventionWeb — knowledge platform
- MCR2030 — cities and local governments
- IRP — recovery
- ARISE — private sector alliance
- DELTA Resilience — data and methodology
- Cross-cutting decision rule

---

## The constant thread

Every sub-brand inherits the following from core UNDRR. None of these flex per sub-brand:

- Roboto + Roboto Condensed typography
- Spacing scale and layout rhythm
- Sentence case headings
- Plain language; no UN jargon; inverted-pyramid structure
- The K / A / C voice trio (Knowledgeable, Approachable, Collaborative)
- UN logo precedence and UNDRR logo safety zone
- Sendai Framework logo on official publications and online sites
- Real people and stories in photography; conference rooms minimum; photo credits always
- British English (Oxford spelling) on external content
- Gender-neutral and disability-inclusive language
- The Mangrove component vocabulary (`mg-` prefix, BEM)

What flexes per sub-brand is documented per section below.

## PreventionWeb — knowledge platform

**Audience.** Researchers, DRR practitioners, national disaster authorities, evidence consumers. Readers come to PreventionWeb expecting depth, citations, and reproducibility. They are time-poor experts who reward content that respects their existing knowledge.

**Register skew.** Knowledgeable + Authoritative. Hopeful and Warm registers play smaller roles — they appear in people-stories and partner spotlights but the spine of the platform is evidence and methodology.

**Visual emphasis.**

- **Primary accent**: PreventionWeb teal, replacing UNDRR Corporate Blue.
- **Photography**: methodology in progress (researchers reviewing maps and printed reports, fieldwork as the work itself, monitoring instruments). Real working artifacts on tables and in hands.
- **Illustration**: methodology diagrams, framework explanations, system maps. Less stylised flourish than core UNDRR; more diagrammatic restraint.
- **Data viz**: prominent, sourced, methodologically transparent. Reduced-saturation Sendai colours for sequential scales; PreventionWeb teal for the primary series.

**What flexes that wouldn't on core UNDRR.** Longer page lengths are acceptable (researchers will read). Citations can be denser. Dataset and report pages can use chart-led heroes rather than people-led heroes.

**Sub-brand-specific anti-patterns.**

- **Avoid**: glowing-network "knowledge platform" stock illustration; abstract "data" backgrounds without specific data; lightbulb iconography.
- **Prefer**: real research artifacts (printed reports, datasets on screens, methodology diagrams, citation-rich tables).

## MCR2030 — cities and local governments

**Audience.** Mayors, municipal planners, community resilience officers, city resilience networks. Readers come to MCR2030 wanting peer recognition, replicable templates, and worked examples from comparable cities.

**Register skew.** Collaborative + Warm. Authoritative remains the floor for any methodology or framework reference, but MCR2030 leans into peer-to-peer voice more than any other sub-brand.

**Visual emphasis.**

- **Primary accent**: MCR2030 purple, replacing UNDRR Corporate Blue.
- **Photography**: cities at varied scales (a small island town next to a mid-sized inland city next to a coastal megacity); municipal staff as protagonists, not central-government figures; the work itself rather than the city skyline.
- **Illustration**: peer-comparison framings (named cities side by side); urban-systems explainers; accessible infrastructure (ramps, tactile paving, accessible evacuation routes) shown as part of the resilient city.
- **Data viz**: city-comparison charts; toolkit visualisations; replicability indicators.

**What flexes that wouldn't on core UNDRR.** First-person plural collective voice ("we as cities," "what local governments are doing") is allowed. Specific named cities can carry the page.

**Sub-brand-specific anti-patterns.**

- **Avoid**: starchitect skylines; single-megacity dominance; "smart city" sci-fi imagery; glass-walled government boardrooms.
- **Prefer**: working municipal offices appropriate to the named city; staff in the field; toolkits and templates as visual subjects.

**AI imagery prompt variant.** See MCR2030 section in <LinkTo kind="brand-ai-imagery-prompts" story="docs">AI imagery prompts</LinkTo>.

## IRP — recovery

**Audience.** Disaster recovery practitioners, national disaster recovery offices, post-disaster planning units. Readers come to IRP expecting the long arc — from initial response through long-term resilience — with practical recovery methodology rather than emergency-response content.

**Register skew.** Hopeful + Authoritative. The combination matters: IRP's voice is grounded in evidence (Authoritative is the floor) but the framing is forward-looking. IRP does not lead with disaster framing.

**Visual emphasis.**

- **Primary accent**: IRP bright blue, replacing UNDRR Corporate Blue.
- **Photography**: recovery in progress, not the disaster — community kitchens in re-opened schools, construction visible, monitoring activity. Two-months-after framing, not the day-after framing.
- **Illustration**: the four-phase recovery framework (response, early recovery, recovery, long-term resilience) as a horizontal flow; methodology diagrams.
- **Data viz**: recovery progress over time; sequential metrics; before-and-during-and-after comparisons (never just before-and-after).

**What flexes that wouldn't on core UNDRR.** A longer time horizon is allowed in storytelling — IRP content can span months and years explicitly. Recovery-specific terminology (early recovery, build-back-better) can sit foregrounded with definitions linked.

**Sub-brand-specific anti-patterns.**

- **Avoid**: ribbon-cutting imagery; before-and-after shock comparisons that exclude the during; distressed survivors; rubble as the dominant frame.
- **Prefer**: agency through recovery action; methodology and monitoring as visible subjects; named partners as protagonists.

**AI imagery prompt variant.** See IRP section in <LinkTo kind="brand-ai-imagery-prompts" story="docs">AI imagery prompts</LinkTo>.

## ARISE — private sector alliance

**Audience.** Companies, insurers, logistics operators, finance institutions, industry associations. Readers come to ARISE wanting a credible business case for risk reduction, partnership models, and continuity benefits framed in terms their boards understand.

**Register skew.** Authoritative + Hopeful with a continuity frame. Of the five sub-brands, ARISE is the most at risk of voice drift — toward business-development, marketing-flavoured, or transactional framings. Authoritative is non-negotiable here for that reason.

**Visual emphasis.**

- **Primary accent**: ARISE burgundy red (#921d2c), replacing UNDRR Corporate Blue.
- **Photography**: industry workers as protagonists, not aid recipients; partnership models (multi-party meetings as peers); operational infrastructure (ports, supply-chain hubs, insurance-relevant assets) as the work happens.
- **Illustration**: business-case visualisations; ROI explainers grounded in real continuity scenarios; named-industry partnership maps.
- **Data viz**: continuity ROI; risk reduction in financial terms; sector-specific impact charts.

**What flexes that wouldn't on core UNDRR.** Financial framings are explicitly allowed (USD figures for avoided continuity disruption, sector-specific risk metrics). Industry-specific terminology can be foregrounded with definitions linked.

**Sub-brand-specific anti-patterns.**

- **Avoid**: an outside expert in a hi-vis vest directing local labour; corporate stock visual clichés (handshake-over-globe, abstract supply-chain swooshes); marketing-flavoured taglines; language that drifts toward business-development voice. Every ARISE artifact must still pass the "would this read on undrr.org" test.
- **Prefer**: local industry workers as protagonists; partnership-model framings; specific evidence with named partners.

**AI imagery prompt variant.** See ARISE section in <LinkTo kind="brand-ai-imagery-prompts" story="docs">AI imagery prompts</LinkTo>.

## DELTA Resilience — data and methodology

**Audience.** Data and methodology specialists, evaluators, monitoring and evaluation officers, researchers working on DRR analytical infrastructure. Readers come to DELTA expecting analytical rigour, transparent methodology, and depth that PreventionWeb covers more broadly.

**Register skew.** Authoritative as the dominant register. Knowledgeable is constant; Hopeful and Warm appear sparingly and only where evidence supports them. DELTA reads more like a methodology journal than the other sub-brands.

**Visual emphasis.**

- **Primary accent**: DELTA dark navy, replacing UNDRR Corporate Blue.
- **Photography**: real methodological artifacts (assessment frameworks on paper, monitoring matrices, validation steps); analysts and evaluators in the work, not at decorative laptops.
- **Illustration**: methodology diagrams; analytical workflows; framework decompositions.
- **Data viz**: highest-density viz of the five sub-brands. Source citations required, methodology footnotes expected.

**What flexes that wouldn't on core UNDRR.** Longer methodology pages and explicit appendix sections are acceptable. Technical terminology can sit foregrounded.

**Sub-brand-specific anti-patterns.**

- **Avoid**: glowing data-visualisation tropes; AI-generic "researcher with laptop" stock; decorative analytics imagery without underlying methodology.
- **Prefer**: real analytical artifacts; methodology in progress; evaluators and researchers as the people in frame.

## Cross-cutting decision rule

When you are about to design or write something for a sub-brand, ask:

1. **Which sub-brand is this for?** Name it. If the answer is "core UNDRR with sub-brand colour," the artifact may belong on undrr.org.
2. **What does the sub-brand's audience need from this artifact?** Read the audience section above.
3. **Which register skew applies?** Use the register skew above as a starting point; pressure-test against the actual content.
4. **What visual emphasis distinguishes this sub-brand from the others?** If you cannot answer in one sentence, the artifact has not yet earned its sub-brand identity.
5. **Does the artifact still read as UN-family if the sub-brand colour is stripped?** It must.
