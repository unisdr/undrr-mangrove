# AI imagery prompts

Generative image and language models produce on-brand work only when the prompt is specific. This page is a working library of prompt templates that translate the UNDRR <LinkTo kind="brand-visual-voice" story="docs">visual voice</LinkTo> and <LinkTo kind="brand-written-voice" story="docs">written voice</LinkTo> into instructions a model can follow.

<HighlightBox variant="primary">
  <strong>The standing rules — read before any prompt below.</strong>
  <ol>
    <li><strong>No photorealistic AI of human beings.</strong> AI imagery may depict people only in clearly stylised forms — flat vector, line illustration, editorial illustration. The output must not read as photographic. This aligns with AP, Reuters, and Guardian editorial standards on AI in journalism, and with the BBC's permissive lane for stylised graphics. For real publication imagery of real people, commission credited photography.</li>
    <li><strong>Never use AI imagery for named people or specific real events.</strong> Use AI for editorial illustration, conceptual diagrams, infographic backgrounds, and concept exploration only.</li>
    <li><strong>Never instruct a model to generate identifiable human faces.</strong> Models cannot generate "named individuals" — they fabricate realistic faces that may match real people in their training data, without consent. Even in stylised illustration, ask for faces in profile, three-quarter view, in motion, or without rendered detail.</li>
    <li><strong>Never generate identifiable images of children.</strong> For child-focused topics, use illustration with no human figures, or commission credited photography with documented consent.</li>
    <li><strong>Specify a named country, not a region.</strong> "Pacific Islands" or "sub-Saharan Africa" produce cultural pastiche. Pick one named place per generation (for example, "Vanuatu, Efate Island," "Kenya, Mombasa County") and a specific built-environment cue.</li>
    <li><strong>Disability inclusion is required, not optional, in every people-inclusive prompt.</strong> The Sendai Framework explicitly addresses disability-inclusive DRR. Ask for at least one disabled person as a participant (not a recipient) — for example, a wheelchair user among community planners, a sign language interpreter at a briefing, a participant using a white cane near an early-warning siren.</li>
    <li><strong>Decolonial framing.</strong> Avoid "outside expert / local recipient" prompt structures. Frame people as peers with named professional roles. The unmarked party in a prompt is the one most likely to default to white/male in model output.</li>
    <li><strong>Diversity must be specified, not implied.</strong> "Diverse" alone is a weak token. Name what you want: skin-tone range including dark brown, varied ages including older adults, varied body types, varied hair textures including textured and curly hair worn naturally.</li>
    <li><strong>Label AI-generated imagery on publication.</strong> UNDRR's editorial standard requires credit and source for every image; generative work surfaces its provenance the same way. <a href="https://contentcredentials.org">C2PA Content Credentials</a> (cryptographic provenance manifests) are an emerging standard worth exploring as platform support grows; adopt opportunistically where the publishing tool supports it. This is not yet a UNDRR-wide commitment.</li>
  </ol>
</HighlightBox>

## How to use this page

Each prompt block contains:

1. **The prompt** — copy-paste into your image or language model
2. **What to expect** — the kind of output that should come back
3. **What to reject** — outputs that fail the brand thread

After generating, run the result through the <LinkTo kind="brand-anti-patterns" story="docs">anti-patterns</LinkTo> checklist before using it anywhere.

## Stylised illustration of people (no photo-realistic AI of humans)

> **Photorealistic AI of human beings is not permitted on UNDRR properties.** This aligns with editorial-standard practice at AP, Reuters, and the Guardian (no AI photoreal in journalism), with the BBC's permissive lane for stylised graphics and illustrations, and with sector guidance from Bond and Oxfam. AI imagery may depict people only in clearly stylised forms — flat vector, line illustration, editorial illustration. The output must not read as photographic. For real publication imagery of real people, commission credited photography per the SharePoint Photography & Icons rule.
>
> **Substitute the bracketed values for each generation.** The named place and built-environment cue are intentional — without them models default to cultural pastiche.

### Stylised illustration — community-led prevention

> Flat vector editorial illustration of a coastal community planting mangrove seedlings in late afternoon light. Setting: [named country and locality, with one specific built-environment cue — for example, "Vanuatu, Efate Island, concrete-block community hall with corrugated-iron roof in mid-ground"]. Clearly stylised, not photo-realistic; flat fills and soft outlines, no skin-tone gradients that mimic photography, no rendered facial detail, no rendered eyes. Faces shown in profile, three-quarter view, or obscured by working posture; never frontal portraits. Mixed team across deliberately varied skin tones (full range including dark brown), varied ages including older adults, varied body types and hair textures including textured and curly hair worn naturally; at least one participant is a disabled person taking part as a peer (for example, a wheelchair user planting from a seated position, or a participant with a white cane on the path). Real working clothing appropriate to the place; no aid-worker hi-vis vests or branded uniforms. Palette: Sendai colours at reduced saturation plus UNDRR Corporate Blue as the primary accent. 16:6 aspect ratio for hero banner, focal subject left of centre, hopeful but grounded mood, no text overlay.

What to expect: a stylised hero illustration with people doing the work in a specifically named place. Composition leaves negative space on the right for editorial copy.

What to reject: any output that reads as photo-realistic (rendered eyes, photographic skin-tone gradient, photographic lighting); hard-hat stock figures; decorative globes; unfamiliar landscapes that could be anywhere; posed compositions looking at the viewer; single-region pastiche when the country was specified.

### Stylised illustration — recovery in progress

> Flat vector editorial illustration, two months after a coastal cyclone, community kitchen serving a meal in a re-opened school. Setting: [named country and locality]. Clearly stylised, not photo-realistic; flat fills and soft outlines, no rendered facial detail, no rendered eyes. Mixed team — at least one man and one older adult cooking actively; women shown leading or coordinating, not only serving. No children in the frame as focal subjects. Faces shown in profile, three-quarter view, or in motion; never frontal portraits. Deliberately varied skin tones, ages, and body types; at least one disabled person present as a participant. No destruction in the foreground; no exposed personal belongings or improvised shelters as focal subject; no expressions of distress, grief, or shock; no comparison to a "before" state in the same frame. Agency conveyed through purposeful action. Palette: Sendai colours at reduced saturation plus UNDRR Corporate Blue as the primary accent. 16:6 aspect ratio, hopeful but factual mood.

What to expect: a stylised illustration of recovery in progress — forward-looking framing, named place, mixed team, dignity preserved through action and stylised abstraction.

What to reject: any output that reads as photo-realistic; rubble as primary subject; helpless figures; distressed faces; children; generic "humanitarian distribution" wide shots; flag-and-banner imagery; anyone in a hi-vis vest directing local labour.

### Stylised illustration — knowledge platform

> Flat vector editorial illustration of two government planners from [named country]'s national disaster management agency reviewing printed flood-risk maps as peers. Mid-shot, daylight working office appropriate to the country (not a glass-walled boardroom). Clearly stylised, not photo-realistic; flat fills and soft outlines, no rendered facial detail, no rendered eyes. Both figures positioned as professional equals — neither shown as instructor or recipient. Mixed gender, varied ages including older adults, deliberately varied skin tones across the full range. At least one of the two figures is a disabled person (for example, a wheelchair user at the table or a participant using a hearing aid). People shown in profile or three-quarter view; never frontal portraits. Recognisable maps and printed reports on the table. Palette: Sendai colours at reduced saturation plus UNDRR Corporate Blue as the primary accent. 16:6 aspect ratio, neutral and authoritative mood, focal subjects centred on the work, no text overlay.

What to expect: a stylised illustration of two professionals as peers with real artifacts on the table. Reads as Knowledgeable + Collaborative without the development-aid frame.

What to reject: any output that reads as photo-realistic; "outside researcher / local recipient" framing; generic boardroom stock; suited figures pointing at laptops; abstract globe-and-data overlays; an unmarked figure (model defaults to white/male) paired with an explicitly "local" figure.

## Editorial illustration

Use these for explainer pages, methodology graphics, infographic backgrounds. Avoid using them where credited photography of real people would do the job better.

### Illustration — early warning systems

> Flat vector editorial illustration depicting an early warning system for coastal flooding in [named country and ecosystem context — for example, "Bangladesh, Sundarbans delta," or "Mozambique, Beira coastal plain"]. Palette: Sendai colours at reduced saturation plus UNDRR Corporate Blue as the primary accent. No human figures. No logos. Side-elevation perspective, not isometric, not 3/4 view. Recognisable elements: sensor buoy at sea, transmission relay tower, community siren, household receiver showing both a visual flashing alert and an audible siren cone, with captioning visible on a TV screen for accessibility. Clear connecting flow lines. White background. Suitable for use behind editorial copy at 16:9 aspect ratio.

What to expect: a clean, technical illustration that explains a system without falling into corporate-isometric clichés or "smart-city" sci-fi tropes. Reads as Knowledgeable. Accessibility cues (visual alert plus captioning) visible alongside the audible siren.

What to reject: AI-generic isometric workplaces, decorative gradients, colour-noise, illegible details, drone or holographic overlays, single-region pastiche when the country was specified, household receiver with audible alert only.

### Illustration — urban resilience

> Flat vector editorial illustration of a city block in [named city, named country] adapting to flooding and heatwaves. Palette: Sendai colours at reduced saturation plus UNDRR Corporate Blue as the primary accent. No human figures. No logos. Side-elevation view. Recognisable elements: green roofs, permeable pavement, shaded street trees, raised infrastructure, cool refuge buildings, accessible step-free routes, tactile paving at crossings, ramps at building entrances. White background. Suitable for an MCR2030 explainer at 16:9 aspect ratio.

What to expect: a clear urban-systems illustration showing recognisable adaptation strategies, including accessibility infrastructure as part of the resilient city.

What to reject: smart-city tropes (drone overlays, holographic data), unrealistic skyline, generic "future city" visuals, accessibility infrastructure missing from the frame.

### Illustration — recovery framework

> Flat vector editorial illustration showing the four phases of disaster recovery (response, early recovery, recovery, long-term resilience) as a horizontal flow. Palette: Sendai colours at reduced saturation plus UNDRR Corporate Blue as the primary accent. No human figures. No logos. Simple line-and-fill style. Recognisable framework elements per phase (assessment, restoration, reconstruction, monitoring). White background. Suitable for an IRP methodology page at 16:9 aspect ratio.

What to expect: a clear flow that earns its place on a methodology page. Reads as Knowledgeable.

What to reject: pie charts pretending to be illustrations, decorative arrows that do not signal flow, gradient blobs without meaning.

### Illustration — disability-inclusive evacuation drill

> Flat vector editorial illustration of a community evacuation drill in [named city, named country]. Setting: a public square or coastal promenade with clear evacuation route signage in the local language script. Mixed group of participants — wheelchair users and people with white canes shown as drill leaders coordinating the route, sign language interpreter at a briefing point, evacuation buddy pairs (one disabled person, one non-disabled). Mixed gender, varied ages including older adults, deliberately varied skin tones across the full range. Recognisable elements: accessible evacuation signage with both pictograms and text, tactile paving along the route, a designated accessible assembly point with shade. Palette: Sendai colours at reduced saturation plus UNDRR Corporate Blue as the primary accent. No identifiable faces (figures shown in motion or in profile). White background. 16:9 aspect ratio.

What to expect: a community evacuation drill with disability inclusion as the framing, not the afterthought. Reads as Approachable + Collaborative.

What to reject: disabled participants shown only as recipients of help; medical or charity-poster framing; any single phenotype dominant.

### Illustration — indigenous-led DRR

> Flat vector editorial illustration of indigenous community-led [DRR practice — for example, "traditional savanna fire management," "mangrove guardianship," "watershed stewardship"] in [named region, named country, named ecosystem]. Indigenous practitioners shown as the protagonists carrying out the practice; no outside-expert figure in frame; no instructor/recipient framing. Mixed gender, varied ages including older adults. Working clothing only — do not generate ceremonial dress, regalia, or culturally specific iconography. People shown in mid-action, faces partially turned or in profile, no frontal portraits, no identifiable faces. Recognisable ecosystem cues drawn from the named region (do not generate generic tropical or generic forest scenery). Palette: Sendai colours at reduced saturation plus UNDRR Corporate Blue as the primary accent. White background. 16:9 aspect ratio.

What to expect: indigenous-led practice shown as expertise, with the ecosystem and country specifically rendered. Reads as Knowledgeable + Collaborative.

What to reject: ceremonial regalia, cultural pastiche from a different region, an outside expert in the frame, any "teaching the locals" framing, faces sharp enough to be recognisable.

### Illustration — gender-and-disasters

> Flat vector editorial illustration of a women-led DRR planning meeting in [named country]. Women shown as decision-makers — speaking, pointing at maps, signing documents; not as audience or note-takers. Mixed ages including older women; men present as participants but not as facilitators. Deliberately varied skin tones across the full range, varied body types, varied hair textures. At least one participant is a disabled woman — for example, a wheelchair user at the table or a participant using a hearing aid. People in profile or three-quarter view; no frontal portraits; no identifiable faces. Recognisable elements: maps, planning documents, working office appropriate to the country (not a glass-walled boardroom). Palette: Sendai colours at reduced saturation plus UNDRR Corporate Blue as the primary accent. White background. 16:9 aspect ratio.

What to expect: women shown as DRR decision-makers, with disability inclusion built in. Reads as Knowledgeable + Collaborative + Hopeful.

What to reject: women shown serving food, taking notes, or in care roles only; men as facilitators with women as audience; single-phenotype groups; identifiable faces.

## Infographic and data viz prompts

For chart styling and infographic backgrounds rather than data — UNDRR data viz uses the in-tree chart components (`stories/Components/BarChart`, `Map`, etc.) for actual data display.

### Infographic background — Sendai progress

> Vector infographic background, single horizontal band, palette: Sendai colours at reduced saturation, no foreground elements, very subtle texture or gradient suggesting progress over time, suitable as a background behind a sequential data visualisation about Sendai Framework targets, low-contrast so foreground type and chart elements remain readable, 16:6 aspect ratio.

What to expect: an unobtrusive backdrop that signals UNDRR brand without competing with chart content.

What to reject: anything that calls attention to itself, busy patterns, gradients that fight the data.

## Sub-brand-specific variants

> **Sub-brand variants inherit every standing rule above.** Named place, no identifiable faces, no children, disability inclusion, deliberately varied skin tones across the full range, no decolonial-aid framing. Do not drop these rules when layering sub-brand colour. The variants below are full additive prompt blocks, not snippets — re-state the standing rules in your final prompt.

The variants below are working hypotheses pending review by UNDRR Communications (`undrrcomms@un.org`). Each sub-brand has a distinct audience and mission; the visual register should follow.

### PreventionWeb (knowledge platform)

> Replace "UNDRR Corporate Blue" in the base prompt with "PreventionWeb teal as the primary accent." Audience emphasis: researchers, practitioners, national disaster authorities. Visual register: Knowledgeable. Show real working artifacts (printed reports, datasets on screens, methodology diagrams, citation-rich tables) rather than abstract "knowledge" visuals (glowing networks, lightbulbs, abstract data). Avoid: generic boardroom or research-lab stock.

### MCR2030 (cities and local governments)

> Replace "UNDRR Corporate Blue" in the base prompt with "MCR2030 purple as the primary accent." Audience emphasis: mayors, municipal planners, community leaders. Visual register: Collaborative, warm, peer-recognising. Show plural cities of varied scale, peer-comparison framings (named cities side by side), municipal staff as protagonists (not central-government figures). Avoid: starchitect skylines, single-megacity dominance, "smart city" sci-fi imagery.

### IRP (recovery)

> Replace "UNDRR Corporate Blue" in the base prompt with "IRP bright blue as the primary accent." Audience emphasis: recovery practitioners, national disaster recovery offices. Visual register: Hopeful but grounded — the long arc from response to long-term resilience. Show recovery in progress, not the disaster; show monitoring and methodology, not just rebuilt buildings. Avoid: ribbon-cutting imagery, before-and-after shock comparisons, distressed survivors.

### ARISE (private sector)

> Replace "UNDRR Corporate Blue" in the base prompt with "ARISE accent colour as the primary accent." Audience emphasis: companies, insurers, logistics, finance, industry associations. Visual register: Authoritative + Hopeful — continuity, partnership, peer recognition. Show local industry workers as protagonists, not as recipients of foreign expertise; show partnership models rather than transactional aid framings. Avoid: an outside expert in a hi-vis vest directing local labour; corporate stock visual clichés (handshake-over-globe, abstract supply-chain swooshes); language or imagery that drifts toward business-development voice. The ARISE artifact must still pass the "would this read on undrr.org" test.

### DELTA Resilience

> Replace "UNDRR Corporate Blue" in the base prompt with "DELTA dark navy as the primary accent." Audience emphasis: data and methodology specialists, evaluators, researchers. Visual register: Authoritative — rigour, transparency, analytical depth. Show real methodological artifacts (assessment frameworks, monitoring matrices, validation steps); show people in the work as analysts and evaluators rather than "data scientists at a screen." Avoid: glowing data-visualisation tropes, AI-generic "researcher with laptop" stock.

## Language model prompts (copy and editorial)

Use these prompt templates with a language model when drafting headlines, captions, alt text, or summaries.

### Alt text generator

> Write alt text for the following image in ≤120 characters. UNDRR house style: descriptive, names people if known, names location if known, names what they are doing, no decorative adjectives, plain English. Image description: [paste].

What to expect: short, factual alt text usable on the web. Names where possible, action-led.

What to reject: alt text that starts with "Image of" / "Photo of" (UN/Microsoft a11y guidance — assume the reader knows it is an image).

### Sentence-case rewriter

> Rewrite the following heading in sentence case per UNDRR house style. Capitalise only the first word, proper nouns, and acronyms. Do not change the meaning. Heading: [paste].

### British-English converter

> Convert the following text from American English to British English (Oxford spelling) per UNDRR Policies and SOPs. Preserve all proper nouns, organisation names, indigenous and place names, transliterations from non-Latin scripts, diacritics (Māori macrons, Spanish accents, Arabic transliteration choices), and quoted speech exactly. Text: [paste].

### Tone-register rewriter

> Rewrite the following copy in **[ONE register]**, where the registers are defined as:
>
> - **Authoritative** — sourced (cite the report, resolution, or dataset by name and date), neutral (state what the evidence shows; let the reader draw the conclusion), precise (full names of frameworks and reports), no institutional fog ("it is important to note that"). For policy summaries, research findings, methodology, official statements.
> - **Warm** — names people, places, jobs, ages; specific sensory detail; quoted speech in people's own words; emotion at the edges. For people stories, community profiles, partner spotlights, recovery narratives.
> - **Hopeful** — forward-looking and evidence-based; collective ("communities," "partners," "member states" named); specific futures ("by 2030," not "in the coming years"); never glosses over what is still hard. For prevention success, recovery progress, anniversary content.
> - **Urgent** — front-loaded (verb in first six words); imperative; concrete (specific actions, times, places); restrained (urgency from precision, not adjectives). For live disasters, early warnings, time-sensitive calls to action.
>
> Hold the K / A / C voice trio (Knowledgeable, Approachable, Collaborative) regardless of register. Use British English (Oxford spelling). Use sentence case for any headings. Avoid jargon. Em-dashes are fine in body prose; do not use them in UI strings, button labels, or story names. Copy: [paste].

### Headline generator

> Generate three headline options in sentence case for a [news / explainer / event / methodology] page about [topic]. UNDRR house style: front-load the key fact, name a place if possible, name a number if available, no marketing-flavoured language, British English (Oxford spelling). Examples of preferred style: "Cyclone deaths halved across Pacific early warning network." "Eighty hectares of mangrove kept Talcahuano-Sur's port open during the March cyclone."

## Pre-publish checklist

Before using any AI-generated output, check it against:

**Standing rules (must hold):**

- [ ] The output is clearly stylised, not photo-realistic. Any output that reads as photographic of human beings is a fail — regenerate or commission credited photography instead.
- [ ] No identifiable human faces in the output. If a face reads as recognisable, regenerate.
- [ ] No identifiable images of children.
- [ ] If the prompt depicts people, the output includes the disability inclusion you specified, deliberately varied skin tones across the full range, and varied ages.
- [ ] No "outside expert / local recipient" framing.
- [ ] The output will be labelled as AI-generated on publication. Where the publishing tool supports it, attach C2PA Content Credentials (opportunistic; not yet a UNDRR-wide requirement).

**Brand thread:**

- [ ] Does it lean into Knowledgeable, Approachable, or Collaborative? (Ideally more than one.)
- [ ] Does it avoid all <LinkTo kind="brand-anti-patterns" story="docs">anti-patterns</LinkTo>?
- [ ] Does it respect the layered identity? (At what layer does it live? Is the thread to the layer above intact?)
- [ ] Does the colour serve meaning, or fill space?
- [ ] Is the content factually accurate? Cross-check against UNDRR sources.

If any standing-rule answer is no, **do not publish** — regenerate with a tighter prompt or commission credited human work instead. If a brand-thread answer is no, regenerate with a tighter prompt or escalate to UNDRR Communications (`undrrcomms@un.org`).
