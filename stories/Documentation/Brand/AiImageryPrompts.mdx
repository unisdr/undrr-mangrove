# AI imagery prompts

Generative image and language models produce on-brand work only when the prompt is specific. This page is a working library of prompt templates that translate the UNDRR <LinkTo kind="brand-visual-voice" story="docs">visual voice</LinkTo> and <LinkTo kind="brand-written-voice" story="docs">written voice</LinkTo> into instructions a model can follow.

<HighlightBox variant="primary">
  <strong>The standing rules — read before any prompt below.</strong>
  <ol>
    <li><strong>No photorealistic AI of human beings.</strong> AI imagery may depict people only in clearly stylised forms — flat vector, line illustration, editorial illustration. The output must not read as photographic. This aligns with AP, Reuters, and Guardian editorial standards on AI in journalism, and with the BBC's permissive lane for stylised graphics. For real publication imagery of real people, commission credited photography.</li>
    <li><strong>Never use AI imagery for named people or specific real events.</strong> Use AI for editorial illustration, conceptual diagrams, infographic backgrounds, and concept exploration only.</li>
    <li><strong>Never instruct a model to generate identifiable human faces.</strong> Models cannot generate "named individuals" — they fabricate realistic faces that may match real people in their training data, without consent. Even in stylised illustration, ask for faces in profile, three-quarter view, in motion, or without rendered detail.</li>
    <li><strong>Never generate identifiable images of children.</strong> For child-focused topics, use illustration with no human figures, or commission credited photography with documented consent.</li>
    <li><strong>No rendered text or standalone icons in AI outputs.</strong> AI image models render text as gibberish (mangled letters that look like writing but aren't), and they invent icons that drift from the OCHA + Mangrove icon systems. Every prompt should include "no text, no rendered letters, no logos, no icons." If signage is conceptually needed in the frame, request "pictogram-only signs without rendered words." Add real text, captions, and icons in your design tool (Figma, Illustrator) <em>after</em> generation, never inside the AI output.</li>
    <li><strong>Specify a named country, not a region.</strong> "Pacific Islands" or "sub-Saharan Africa" produce cultural pastiche. Pick one named place per generation (for example, "Vanuatu, Efate Island," "Kenya, Mombasa County") and a specific built-environment cue.</li>
    <li><strong>Diversity within a named place — name what kind of gathering this is.</strong> The named-country rule and the diversity rule pull against each other in single-country contexts: when you name "Bangladesh," the model defaults to a single ethnic phenotype even when you ask for varied skin tones. Resolve this by being explicit about the type of gathering. <strong>Local community context</strong> (a single village, a specific community-led activity): ethnic homogeneity may be appropriate to the place; diversity should come through age, gender, ability, and role — not through forced ethnic mixing of a community that does not in fact look that way. <strong>National-government context in a country with significant within-country diversity</strong>: name the heritage groups explicitly — for example, "Bangladeshi government planners reflecting Bangladesh's ethnic diversity — participants of Bengali, Adivasi, and Chittagong Hill Tracts heritage," or for Brazil "of Afro-Brazilian, Indigenous, European-Brazilian, and Asian-Brazilian heritage." Ask for the diversity that actually exists in that place. <strong>Regional or international convening hosted in a named country</strong>: name the participating countries — for example, "South Asian regional DRR planning meeting hosted in Dhaka, with participants from Bangladesh, Bhutan, India, Maldives, Nepal, Pakistan, and Sri Lanka." Name the nature of the gathering, not just the host country.</li>
    <li><strong>Disability inclusion is required, not optional, in every people-inclusive prompt.</strong> The Sendai Framework explicitly addresses disability-inclusive DRR. Ask for at least one disabled person as a participant (not a recipient) — for example, a wheelchair user among community planners, a sign language interpreter at a briefing, a participant using a white cane near an early-warning siren.</li>
    <li><strong>Decolonial framing.</strong> Avoid "outside expert / local recipient" prompt structures. Frame people as peers with named professional roles. The unmarked party in a prompt is the one most likely to default to white/male in model output.</li>
    <li><strong>Diversity must be specified, not implied — and varied within the named place.</strong> "Diverse" alone is a weak token. Name what you want: skin-tone range across the range relevant to the named place, varied ages including older adults, varied body types, varied hair textures including textured and curly hair worn naturally. Vary clothing within regional norms — not all participants in identical dress.</li>
    <li><strong>Use specific hex codes for colours.</strong> "Sendai colours" alone means nothing to an image model. The full palette is: <code>#c10920</code> Sendai red, <code>#eb752a</code> Sendai orange, <code>#962987</code> Sendai purple, <code>#00afae</code> Sendai turquoise. Reduced-saturation versions: <code>#d45363</code>, <code>#f19e6a</code>, <code>#b669ab</code>, <code>#4dc7c6</code>. Plus <code>#004f91</code> UNDRR Corporate Blue. Sub-brand primaries: <code>#0a6969</code> PreventionWeb teal, <code>#591a61</code> MCR2030 purple, <code>#0f78bf</code> IRP bright blue, <code>#132e48</code> DELTA dark navy. Always include the actual hex values in prompts, not just the colour name.</li>
    <li><strong>Label AI-generated imagery on publication.</strong> UNDRR's editorial standard requires credit and source for every image; generative work surfaces its provenance the same way. <a href="https://contentcredentials.org">C2PA Content Credentials</a> (cryptographic provenance manifests) are an emerging standard worth exploring as platform support grows; adopt opportunistically where the publishing tool supports it. This is not yet a UNDRR-wide commitment.</li>
  </ol>
</HighlightBox>

## How to use this page

Each prompt block contains:

1. **The prompt** — copy-paste into your image or language model
2. **What to expect** — the kind of output that should come back
3. **What to reject** — outputs that fail the brand thread

After generating, run the result through the <LinkTo kind="brand-anti-patterns" story="docs">anti-patterns</LinkTo> checklist before using it anywhere.

---

## Stylised illustration of people (no photo-realistic AI of humans)

> **Photorealistic AI of human beings is not permitted on UNDRR properties.** This aligns with editorial-standard practice at AP, Reuters, and the Guardian (no AI photoreal in journalism), with the BBC's permissive lane for stylised graphics and illustrations, and with sector guidance from Bond and Oxfam. AI imagery may depict people only in clearly stylised forms — flat vector, line illustration, editorial illustration. The output must not read as photographic. For real publication imagery of real people, commission credited photography per the SharePoint Photography & Icons rule.
>
> **Substitute the bracketed values for each generation.** The named place and built-environment cue are intentional — without them models default to cultural pastiche.

### Stylised illustration — community-led prevention

> Flat vector editorial illustration of a coastal community planting mangrove seedlings in late afternoon light. Setting: [named country and locality, with one specific built-environment cue — for example, "Vanuatu, Efate Island, concrete-block community hall with corrugated-iron roof in mid-ground"]. Clearly stylised, not photo-realistic; flat fills and soft outlines, no rendered facial detail. Faces in profile, three-quarter view, or obscured by working posture; never frontal portraits. Mixed team: deliberately varied skin tones, ages including older adults, body types; at least one disabled participant as a peer. Working clothing appropriate to the place; no aid-worker hi-vis vests. Palette: Sendai reduced-saturation (#d45363, #f19e6a, #b669ab, #4dc7c6) plus UNDRR Corporate Blue (#004f91). 16:6 hero banner, focal subject left of centre, hopeful mood, no text, no rendered letters, no logos, no icons.

What to expect: a stylised hero illustration with people doing the work in a specifically named place. Composition leaves negative space on the right for editorial copy.

What to reject: any output that reads as photo-realistic (rendered eyes, photographic skin-tone gradient, photographic lighting); hard-hat stock figures; decorative globes; unfamiliar landscapes that could be anywhere; posed compositions looking at the viewer; single-region pastiche when the country was specified.

### Stylised illustration — recovery in progress

> Flat vector editorial illustration two months after a coastal cyclone: community kitchen serving a meal in a re-opened school. Setting: [named country and locality]. Clearly stylised, flat fills and soft outlines, no rendered facial detail. Mixed team — at least one man and one older adult cooking; women leading, not only serving. No children as focal subjects. No destruction, exposed belongings, or distress in frame. Deliberately varied skin tones, ages, body types; at least one disabled participant as a peer. Palette: Sendai reduced-saturation (#d45363, #f19e6a, #b669ab, #4dc7c6) plus UNDRR Corporate Blue (#004f91). 16:6 aspect ratio, hopeful but factual mood, no text, no rendered letters, no logos, no icons.

What to expect: a stylised illustration of recovery in progress — forward-looking framing, named place, mixed team, dignity preserved through action and stylised abstraction.

What to reject: any output that reads as photo-realistic; rubble as primary subject; helpless figures; distressed faces; children; generic "humanitarian distribution" wide shots; flag-and-banner imagery; anyone in a hi-vis vest directing local labour.

### Stylised illustration — knowledge platform

> Flat vector editorial illustration of two government planners from [named country]'s disaster management agency reviewing printed flood-risk maps as peers. Setting: [working office appropriate to the country — not a glass-walled boardroom]. Clearly stylised, flat fills and soft outlines, no rendered facial detail. Both figures as professional equals; mixed gender, varied ages including older adults, deliberately varied skin tones; at least one disabled person. Faces in profile or three-quarter view. Recognisable maps and reports on the table. Palette: Sendai reduced-saturation (#d45363, #f19e6a, #b669ab, #4dc7c6) plus UNDRR Corporate Blue (#004f91). 16:6 aspect ratio, authoritative mood, focal subjects centred on the work, no text, no rendered letters, no logos, no icons.

What to expect: a stylised illustration of two professionals as peers with real artifacts on the table. Reads as Knowledgeable + Collaborative without the development-aid frame.

What to reject: any output that reads as photo-realistic; "outside researcher / local recipient" framing; generic boardroom stock; suited figures pointing at laptops; abstract globe-and-data overlays; an unmarked figure (model defaults to white/male) paired with an explicitly "local" figure.

## Editorial illustration

Use these for explainer pages, methodology graphics, infographic backgrounds. Avoid using them where credited photography of real people would do the job better.

### Illustration — early warning systems

> Flat vector editorial illustration of an early warning system for coastal flooding in [named country and ecosystem — e.g. "Bangladesh, Sundarbans delta"]. No human figures. Side-elevation perspective, not isometric. Elements: sensor buoy at sea, transmission relay tower, community siren, household receiver showing a flashing visual alert symbol and audible siren waves, TV screen with a closed-captioning pictogram (no rendered words). Clear connecting flow lines. White background. Palette: Sendai reduced-saturation (#d45363, #f19e6a, #b669ab, #4dc7c6) plus UNDRR Corporate Blue (#004f91). 16:9 aspect ratio, no text, no rendered letters, no logos, no icons.

What to expect: a clean, technical illustration that explains a system without falling into corporate-isometric clichés or "smart-city" sci-fi tropes. Reads as Knowledgeable. Accessibility cues (flashing visual alert plus captioning pictogram) visible alongside the audible siren.

What to reject: AI-generic isometric workplaces, decorative gradients, colour-noise, illegible details, drone or holographic overlays, single-region pastiche when the country was specified, household receiver with audible alert only.

### Illustration — urban resilience

> Flat vector editorial illustration of a city block in [named city, named country] adapting to flooding and heatwaves. Palette: Sendai colours at reduced saturation (#d45363 red, #f19e6a orange, #b669ab purple, #4dc7c6 turquoise) plus UNDRR Corporate Blue (#004f91) as the primary accent. No human figures. No text, no rendered letters, no logos, no icons. Side-elevation view. Recognisable elements: green roofs, permeable pavement, shaded street trees, raised infrastructure, cool refuge buildings, accessible step-free routes, tactile paving at crossings, ramps at building entrances. White background. Suitable for an MCR2030 explainer at 16:9 aspect ratio.

What to expect: a clear urban-systems illustration showing recognisable adaptation strategies, including accessibility infrastructure as part of the resilient city.

What to reject: smart-city tropes (drone overlays, holographic data), unrealistic skyline, generic "future city" visuals, accessibility infrastructure missing from the frame.

### Illustration — recovery framework

> Flat vector editorial illustration showing the four phases of disaster recovery (response, early recovery, recovery, long-term resilience) as a horizontal flow. Palette: Sendai colours at reduced saturation (#d45363 red, #f19e6a orange, #b669ab purple, #4dc7c6 turquoise) plus UNDRR Corporate Blue (#004f91) as the primary accent. No human figures. No text, no rendered letters, no logos, no icons. Simple line-and-fill style. Recognisable framework elements per phase (assessment, restoration, reconstruction, monitoring). White background. Suitable for an IRP methodology page at 16:9 aspect ratio.

What to expect: a clear flow that earns its place on a methodology page. Reads as Knowledgeable.

What to reject: pie charts pretending to be illustrations, decorative arrows that do not signal flow, gradient blobs without meaning.

### Illustration — disability-inclusive evacuation drill

> Flat vector editorial illustration of a community evacuation drill in [named city, named country]. Setting: public square or coastal promenade. Mixed group: wheelchair users and white-cane users as drill leaders, sign language interpreter at a briefing point, evacuation buddy pairs (disabled and non-disabled). Mixed gender, varied ages including older adults, deliberately varied skin tones appropriate to the named place; varied clothing within regional norms. Pictogram-only evacuation signs (no rendered words), tactile paving, accessible assembly point with shade. Faces in motion or profile — no identifiable faces. Palette: Sendai reduced-saturation (#d45363, #f19e6a, #b669ab, #4dc7c6) plus UNDRR Corporate Blue (#004f91). White background. 16:9 aspect ratio, no text, no rendered letters, no logos, no icons.

What to expect: a community evacuation drill with disability inclusion as the framing, not the afterthought. Reads as Approachable + Collaborative.

What to reject: disabled participants shown only as recipients of help; medical or charity-poster framing; any single phenotype dominant; signs with rendered words (gibberish letters); the sign language interpreter signing nothing or shown as decorative.

### Illustration — indigenous-led DRR

> Flat vector editorial illustration of indigenous community-led [DRR practice — e.g. "traditional savanna fire management," "mangrove guardianship," "watershed stewardship"] in [named region, country, ecosystem]. Indigenous practitioners as protagonists; no outside-expert figure; no instructor/recipient framing. Mixed gender, varied ages. Working clothing only — no ceremonial dress or regalia. Ecosystem cues drawn from the named region; do not generate generic tropical or forest scenery. Faces in profile or partially turned; no frontal portraits. Palette: Sendai reduced-saturation (#d45363, #f19e6a, #b669ab, #4dc7c6) plus UNDRR Corporate Blue (#004f91). White background. 16:9 aspect ratio, no text, no rendered letters, no logos, no icons.

What to expect: indigenous-led practice shown as expertise, with the ecosystem and country specifically rendered. Reads as Knowledgeable + Collaborative.

What to reject: ceremonial regalia, cultural pastiche from a different region, an outside expert in the frame, any "teaching the locals" framing, faces sharp enough to be recognisable.

### Illustration — gender-and-disasters

> **Pick the framing first.** This prompt has three variants depending on the kind of meeting you mean. The first variant is the most common failure: the model collapses a single named country into a single ethnic phenotype, even when the prompt asks for varied skin tones. Pick the variant that matches your story.

**Variant A — within-country diversity (national-government context).** Use when the meeting is a national disaster authority planning unit and you want to show within-country heritage diversity.

> Flat vector editorial illustration of a women-led DRR planning meeting in [named country]'s national disaster management authority. Setting: working government office, not a glass-walled boardroom. Participants reflect [named country]'s ethnic and heritage diversity — e.g. for Bangladesh: women of Bengali Muslim, Bengali Hindu, Adivasi, and Chittagong Hill Tracts heritage. Varied clothing within regional norms. Women as decision-makers — speaking, pointing at maps; men present as participants, not facilitators. Mixed ages; at least one disabled woman. Faces in profile or three-quarter view; no frontal portraits. Maps and planning documents on the table. Palette: Sendai reduced-saturation (#d45363, #f19e6a, #b669ab, #4dc7c6) plus UNDRR Corporate Blue (#004f91). No text, no rendered letters, no logos. White background. 16:9 aspect ratio.

**Variant B — regional or international convening (multi-country participation).** Use when the meeting is a regional or international DRR convening hosted in a country.

> Flat vector editorial illustration of a women-led DRR regional planning meeting hosted in [named city, country], with participants from [named region — e.g. "South Asia: Bangladesh, Bhutan, India, Maldives, Nepal, Pakistan, and Sri Lanka"]. Working conference setting. Participants of deliberately varied national heritage from the named region; varied skin tones, varied dress within regional norms, mixed ages including older women. Women as decision-makers — speaking, pointing at maps. At least one disabled woman; faces in profile or three-quarter view. Maps and planning documents. Palette: Sendai reduced-saturation (#d45363, #f19e6a, #b669ab, #4dc7c6) plus UNDRR Corporate Blue (#004f91). No text, no rendered letters, no logos. White background. 16:9 aspect ratio.

**Variant C — local community context (single named place).** Use when the gathering is a community-led activity in a specific village or neighbourhood; ethnic homogeneity may be appropriate to the place.

> Flat vector editorial illustration of a women-led community resilience meeting in [named village or neighbourhood, country]. Participants are members of the named community; ethnic appearance reflects the place — do not force ethnic mixing of a community that does not in fact look that way. Diversity through age (including older women), ability (at least one disabled participant as a peer), body type, and role implied through clothing. Women as decision-makers; not as audience. Faces in profile or three-quarter view. Working space appropriate to the place. Palette: Sendai reduced-saturation (#d45363, #f19e6a, #b669ab, #4dc7c6) plus UNDRR Corporate Blue (#004f91). No text, no rendered letters, no logos. White background. 16:9 aspect ratio.

What to expect: women shown as DRR decision-makers, with the appropriate kind of diversity for the kind of gathering, and disability inclusion built in.

What to reject: women shown serving food, taking notes, or in care roles only; men as facilitators with women as audience; single-phenotype groups when the variant explicitly asked for diversity; identifiable faces; identical dress across all participants.

## Infographic and data viz prompts

For chart styling and infographic backgrounds rather than data — UNDRR data viz uses the in-tree chart components (`stories/Components/BarChart`, `Map`, etc.) for actual data display.

### Infographic background — Sendai progress

> Vector infographic background, single horizontal band, palette: Sendai colours at reduced saturation, no foreground elements, very subtle texture or gradient suggesting progress over time, suitable as a background behind a sequential data visualisation about Sendai Framework targets, low-contrast so foreground type and chart elements remain readable, 16:6 aspect ratio.

What to expect: an unobtrusive backdrop that signals UNDRR brand without competing with chart content.

What to reject: anything that calls attention to itself, busy patterns, gradients that fight the data.

## Sub-brand-specific variants

> **Sub-brand variants inherit every standing rule above.** Named place, no identifiable faces, no children, disability inclusion, deliberately varied skin tones across the full range, no decolonial-aid framing. Do not drop these rules when layering sub-brand colour. The variants below are full additive prompt blocks, not snippets — re-state the standing rules in your final prompt.

Each sub-brand has a distinct audience and mission; the visual register should follow.

### PreventionWeb (knowledge platform)

> Replace "UNDRR Corporate Blue (#004f91)" in the base prompt with "PreventionWeb teal (#0a6969) as the primary accent." Audience emphasis: researchers, practitioners, national disaster authorities. Visual register: Knowledgeable. Show real working artifacts (printed reports, datasets on screens, methodology diagrams, citation-rich tables) rather than abstract "knowledge" visuals (glowing networks, lightbulbs, abstract data). Avoid: generic boardroom or research-lab stock.

### MCR2030 (cities and local governments)

> Replace "UNDRR Corporate Blue (#004f91)" in the base prompt with "MCR2030 purple (#591a61) as the primary accent." Audience emphasis: mayors, municipal planners, community leaders. Visual register: Collaborative, warm, peer-recognising. Show plural cities of varied scale, peer-comparison framings (named cities side by side), municipal staff as protagonists (not central-government figures). Avoid: starchitect skylines, single-megacity dominance, "smart city" sci-fi imagery.

### IRP (recovery)

> Replace "UNDRR Corporate Blue (#004f91)" in the base prompt with "IRP bright blue (#0f78bf) as the primary accent." Audience emphasis: recovery practitioners, national disaster recovery offices. Visual register: Hopeful but grounded — the long arc from response to long-term resilience. Show recovery in progress, not the disaster; show monitoring and methodology, not just rebuilt buildings. Avoid: ribbon-cutting imagery, before-and-after shock comparisons, distressed survivors.

### ARISE (private sector)

> Replace "UNDRR Corporate Blue (#004f91)" in the base prompt with "ARISE accent colour as the primary accent." Audience emphasis: companies, insurers, logistics, finance, industry associations. Visual register: Authoritative + Hopeful — continuity, partnership, peer recognition. Show local industry workers as protagonists, not as recipients of foreign expertise; show partnership models rather than transactional aid framings. Avoid: an outside expert in a hi-vis vest directing local labour; corporate stock visual clichés (handshake-over-globe, abstract supply-chain swooshes); language or imagery that drifts toward business-development voice. The ARISE artifact must still pass the "would this read on undrr.org" test.

### DELTA Resilience

> Replace "UNDRR Corporate Blue (#004f91)" in the base prompt with "DELTA dark navy (#132e48) as the primary accent." Audience emphasis: data and methodology specialists, evaluators, researchers. Visual register: Authoritative — rigour, transparency, analytical depth. Show real methodological artifacts (assessment frameworks, monitoring matrices, validation steps); show people in the work as analysts and evaluators rather than "data scientists at a screen." Avoid: glowing data-visualisation tropes, AI-generic "researcher with laptop" stock.

## Language model prompts (copy and editorial)

Use these prompt templates with a language model when drafting headlines, captions, alt text, or summaries.

### Alt text generator

> Write alt text for the following image in ≤120 characters. UNDRR house style: descriptive, names people if known, names location if known, names what they are doing, no decorative adjectives, plain English. Image description: [paste].

What to expect: short, factual alt text usable on the web. Names where possible, action-led.

What to reject: alt text that starts with "Image of" / "Photo of" (UN/Microsoft a11y guidance — assume the reader knows it is an image).

### Sentence-case rewriter

> Rewrite the following heading in sentence case per UNDRR house style. Capitalise only the first word, proper nouns, and acronyms. Do not change the meaning. Heading: [paste].

### British-English converter

> Convert the following text from American English to British English (Oxford spelling) per UNDRR Policies and SOPs. Preserve all proper nouns, organisation names, indigenous and place names, transliterations from non-Latin scripts, diacritics (Māori macrons, Spanish accents, Arabic transliteration choices), and quoted speech exactly. Text: [paste].

### Tone-register rewriter

> Rewrite the following copy in **[ONE register]**, where the registers are defined as:
>
> - **Authoritative** — sourced (cite the report, resolution, or dataset by name and date), neutral (state what the evidence shows; let the reader draw the conclusion), precise (full names of frameworks and reports), no institutional fog ("it is important to note that"). For policy summaries, research findings, methodology, official statements.
> - **Warm** — names people, places, jobs, ages; specific sensory detail; quoted speech in people's own words; emotion at the edges. For people stories, community profiles, partner spotlights, recovery narratives.
> - **Hopeful** — forward-looking and evidence-based; collective ("communities," "partners," "member states" named); specific futures ("by 2030," not "in the coming years"); never glosses over what is still hard. For prevention success, recovery progress, anniversary content.
> - **Urgent** — front-loaded (verb in first six words); imperative; concrete (specific actions, times, places); restrained (urgency from precision, not adjectives). For live disasters, early warnings, time-sensitive calls to action.
>
> Hold the K / A / C voice trio (Knowledgeable, Approachable, Collaborative) regardless of register. Use British English (Oxford spelling). Use sentence case for any headings. Avoid jargon. Em-dashes are fine in body prose; do not use them in UI strings, button labels, or story names. Copy: [paste].

### Headline generator

> Generate three headline options in sentence case for a [news / explainer / event / methodology] page about [topic]. UNDRR house style: front-load the key fact, name a place if possible, name a number if available, no marketing-flavoured language, British English (Oxford spelling). Examples of preferred style: "Cyclone deaths halved across Pacific early warning network." "Eighty hectares of mangrove kept Talcahuano-Sur's port open during the March cyclone."

## Pre-publish checklist

Before using any AI-generated output, check it against:

**Standing rules (must hold):**

- [ ] The output is clearly stylised, not photo-realistic. Any output that reads as photographic of human beings is a fail — regenerate or commission credited photography instead.
- [ ] No identifiable human faces in the output. If a face reads as recognisable, regenerate.
- [ ] No identifiable images of children.
- [ ] **No rendered text in the output.** Gibberish letters that look like writing but aren't are a fail; regenerate or strip in the design tool. Add real text in Figma / Illustrator after generation.
- [ ] **No standalone icons rendered by the AI** (they will not match the OCHA + Mangrove icon systems). Add real icons in the design tool after generation.
- [ ] If the prompt named a country, does the output reflect the appropriate kind of diversity for that gathering? Check that single-country contexts didn't collapse into a single ethnic phenotype, that participants vary in dress within regional norms, and that the output isn't homogenised in ways the prompt explicitly asked against.
- [ ] If the prompt depicts people, the output includes the disability inclusion you specified, varied ages, varied body types, varied hair textures.
- [ ] No "outside expert / local recipient" framing.
- [ ] The output will be labelled as AI-generated on publication. Where the publishing tool supports it, attach C2PA Content Credentials (opportunistic; not yet a UNDRR-wide requirement).

**Brand thread:**

- [ ] Does it lean into Knowledgeable, Approachable, or Collaborative? (Ideally more than one.)
- [ ] Does it avoid all <LinkTo kind="brand-anti-patterns" story="docs">anti-patterns</LinkTo>?
- [ ] Does it respect the layered identity? (At what layer does it live? Is the thread to the layer above intact?)
- [ ] Does the colour serve meaning, or fill space?
- [ ] Is the content factually accurate? Cross-check against UNDRR sources.

If any standing-rule answer is no, **do not publish** — regenerate with a tighter prompt or commission credited human work instead. If a brand-thread answer is no, regenerate with a tighter prompt or escalate to UNDRR Communications (`undrrcomms@un.org`).
